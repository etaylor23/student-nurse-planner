The fastest way to learn Gibbs is to read one done well. Below is a **complete worked reflection** — a realistic, fully anonymised episode from a student placement, written through all six stages of the Gibbs cycle, with a short annotation after each stage explaining what it's doing and why. Steal the structure; replace the story with yours. (New to the model? Start with the [full how-to guide](/guides/how-to-write-a-nursing-reflection), then come back.)

## The scenario

A second-year student on a medical ward is asked to hand over a bay of four patients at the end of a busy long day — their first time leading handover rather than observing it.

## Stage 1 — Description

> At the end of a long day shift, my practice supervisor asked me to lead the handover for our bay of four patients to the incoming night staff. I had observed handovers regularly but not led one. I handed over using the ward's SBAR template. For three patients this went smoothly. For the fourth, I realised partway through that I could not clearly explain the plan for their intravenous antibiotics, and my supervisor stepped in to complete that section.

**Why this works:** four sentences, facts only, no analysis, nobody identifiable — roles instead of names, no ages, no conditions rare enough to identify anyone. The scene is set and the reflection has a clear centre of gravity: the moment it wobbled.

## Stage 2 — Feelings

> Beforehand I felt nervous but pleased to be trusted. While handing over the first three patients I grew in confidence. When I stumbled on the fourth I felt exposed and embarrassed, particularly in front of the night staff, and I worried they would question my competence. Afterwards I mostly felt frustrated with myself, because I had known about the antibiotic plan during the shift but had not written it down.

**Why this works:** honest, specific, and it names the *change* in feeling across the episode. Notice it already points toward the lesson — the frustration is about preparation, not ability — without jumping ahead to analysis.

## Stage 3 — Evaluation

> What went well: I used the SBAR structure confidently for three of four patients, kept to time, and the night staff asked few clarifying questions afterwards. My supervisor said my communication was clear. What went badly: I relied on memory for the fourth patient's plan rather than my written notes, and the gap appeared at exactly the moment it mattered. It was also good that my supervisor stepped in promptly — the incoming team got accurate information.

**Why this works:** both directions, honestly weighed, and it includes the patient-safety view (the handover was accurate *because* the supervisor intervened) — not just the student's-eye view.

## Stage 4 — Analysis

> The difference between the three smooth handovers and the fourth was not knowledge but preparation: I had written structured notes for three patients and assumed I would remember the fourth. Handover is a known safety-critical communication point, and structured tools like SBAR exist precisely because working memory fails under pressure. My experience matches this: the structure carried me exactly as far as my written preparation did, and no further. Leading for the first time also added cognitive load that observing never had — which explains why something I "knew" became inaccessible mid-handover.

**Why this works:** it reaches for *why*, connects the episode to what the evidence base says about structured communication and cognitive load, and produces a transferable insight — the tool works when the preparation behind it is done.

## Stage 5 — Conclusion

> I learned that my handover communication is stronger than I expected, and that my failure point was preparation rather than delivery. If I faced the same situation again, I would complete written SBAR notes for every patient before handover, regardless of how well I felt I knew them, and I would ask to lead handovers regularly rather than treating this as a one-off test.

**Why this works:** it answers "what else could I have done?" concretely and claims the positive lesson as well as the negative one — reflections that only self-criticise are as unbalanced as ones that only self-congratulate.

## Stage 6 — Action plan

> For the rest of this placement I will: (1) prepare written SBAR notes for every patient in my bay before each handover; (2) ask my supervisor for the opportunity to lead handover at least once a week, with feedback afterwards; and (3) write a follow-up note in four weeks comparing my confidence and accuracy against this episode.

**Why this works:** three actions — specific, scheduled, checkable. "Communicate better" is a wish; "written SBAR notes before every handover" is a behaviour you can audit. The follow-up note closes the loop and gives your next reflection its starting point.

## Using this as a template

The pattern to copy is the proportions: description and feelings kept short, evaluation honest in both directions, the weight landing on **analysis**, and an action plan a stranger could check up on. This whole reflection is about 450 words of actual content — the length most placements expect. A reflection like this also makes strong [proficiency evidence](/guides/how-to-evidence-nmc-proficiencies): this one plausibly supports statements around communication, teamwork and accountable practice.

## Frequently asked questions

**How long should each Gibbs stage be?**
No fixed rule, but the example above is typical: one short paragraph per stage, with analysis the longest. 300–600 words total suits most placement reflections.

**Can I write a Gibbs reflection about something that went well?**
Yes — reflecting on success ("why did that de-escalation work?") is underrated and produces some of the most useful action plans. The cycle is identical.

**Is this example real?**
It's realistic but constructed for teaching, and fully anonymised — which is exactly the standard your own reflections must meet. No names, no identifying details, ever.
