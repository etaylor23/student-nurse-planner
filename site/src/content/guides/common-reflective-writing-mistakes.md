Most weak reflections fail in the same handful of ways — and every one of them is fixable with a small change of habit. Here are the seven mistakes markers and practice assessors see constantly, why each one hollows out a reflection, and the fix. (For the underlying method, see [how to write a nursing reflection](/guides/how-to-write-a-nursing-reflection).)

## 1. All description, no analysis

The classic. Three paragraphs of what happened, one sentence of "I found this really interesting", the end. Description is the cheapest part of a reflection; the learning lives in *why* things unfolded as they did and what that means for your practice.

**Fix:** cap description at a third of the total and interrogate the rest. If your draft never answers a "why?", it isn't finished. The [worked Gibbs example](/guides/gibbs-reflective-cycle-example) shows the proportions done right.

## 2. Identifiable details

Names, initials, ages, wards, dates, distinctive conditions — alone or in combination, they can identify a patient, and they turn a learning exercise into a confidentiality breach. This is the one mistake with professional consequences.

**Fix:** roles, not names ("the patient", "a relative", "my practice supervisor"); strip ages, locations and dates; and if the episode can't make its point without identifying detail, choose a different episode. Reread for this *before* submitting, every time.

## 3. The suspiciously perfect account

Reflections in which the writer read the situation flawlessly, acted decisively and felt only appropriate emotions. Assessors don't believe them, and worse, they contain no learning — a reflection without vulnerability is a report.

**Fix:** write the feeling you actually had, including the unflattering ones. "I froze" and "I felt defensive when corrected" are the raw material of genuinely useful analysis, and honesty reads instantly.

## 4. Borrowed insight

Concluding with what you sense you're *supposed* to have learned — "this taught me the importance of communication" — rather than what you did learn. Generic insight is the tell of a reflection written for the marker, not the writer.

**Fix:** push one level down. Important *how*? What specifically would you now do differently, and what would that change? If your conclusion could be pasted onto any classmate's reflection unedited, it isn't yours yet.

## 5. Vague action plans

"I will improve my time management." Noble, unmeasurable, forgotten by Friday. An action plan that can't be checked isn't a plan — it's a mood.

**Fix:** behaviour + trigger + timeframe. "I will prepare written SBAR notes before every handover for the rest of this placement, and review with my supervisor in week four." A stranger should be able to audit whether you did it.

## 6. Reflecting only on disasters

If every reflection is a crisis post-mortem, you're learning only from your worst days. Success has causes too, and they're the habits you most want to reproduce.

**Fix:** regularly reflect on something that went *well* and work out why. [Driscoll's quick three-question model](/guides/driscolls-model-of-reflection) makes small positive reflections cheap enough to do weekly.

## 7. Writing it weeks later

Memory doesn't keep feelings fresh — reflections reconstructed long after the event turn into fiction with references. The emotional detail that powers stage 2 of Gibbs evaporates within days.

**Fix:** capture two sentences on the day (what happened, how it felt), even if the full write-up comes later. That seed note preserves the honest version. ## The one-minute pre-submission checklist

- Could anyone be identified, directly or by combination of details? → rewrite.
- Is at least half the piece making sense of the episode, rather than retelling it?
- Is there a feeling in it you'd hesitate to admit at handover? (Good — keep it.)
- Does the conclusion say something a classmate's couldn't?
- Could someone check whether you did your action plan?

Five yeses and it's ready.

## Frequently asked questions

**What's the single biggest reflective-writing mistake?**
Description without analysis. If the piece never answers *why*, it's a diary entry — and it'll be marked like one.

**Can I mention colleagues in a reflection?**
By role only ("a staff nurse", "my practice supervisor"), and describe their actions professionally. The confidentiality bar protects colleagues as well as patients.

**Is it okay if my reflection shows I made a mistake?**
Yes — honest reflection on error is exactly what the exercise is for, handled with anonymity and proportion. Anything involving actual harm or safeguarding must also go through proper reporting channels first; a reflection never substitutes for escalation.
