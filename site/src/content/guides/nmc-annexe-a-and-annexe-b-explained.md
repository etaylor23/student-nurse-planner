The seven [NMC platforms](/guides/nmc-platforms-explained) describe what a nurse must know and be; the two annexes describe what a nurse must be able to **do**. **Annexe A** lists the communication and relationship management skills; **Annexe B** lists the nursing procedures, organised into 11 groups. Every statement in both annexes is part of the same [219-statement set](/guides/nmc-proficiencies-and-your-pad) your programme signs off before you can register.

## Why the annexes exist

When the NMC rewrote its standards, it separated the *dimensions* of professional practice (the platforms) from the *concrete skills* those dimensions are exercised through. That separation is useful to you: platform statements are evidenced through judgement and conduct over time, while annexe skills are demonstrable — things a supervisor can watch you do, coach, and sign with confidence. Knowing which kind of statement you're chasing changes how you evidence it.

## Annexe A — communication and relationship management skills

Annexe A covers the interpersonal machinery of nursing, including:

- **Underpinning communication skills** — active listening, clear spoken and written English, plain-language explanation, checking understanding.
- **Working with people across contexts** — communicating with people with anxiety, distress, cognitive impairment, sensory impairment, or where English is not their first language.
- **Therapeutic and difficult conversations** — building trust, motivational conversation, breaking significant news, de-escalation, and supporting people making difficult decisions.
- **Working in teams** — handover, escalation, feedback, and challenge across professional boundaries.

Annexe A skills hide inside ordinary practice, which makes them easy to under-evidence: nobody writes "I actively listened today". The fix is to capture the *charged* communication moments — the angry relative, the frightened pre-op patient, the handover that prevented an error — as [reflections](/guides/how-to-write-a-nursing-reflection), then link them to the specific Annexe A skills they demonstrate.

## Annexe B — nursing procedures

Annexe B is the hands-on list, organised into **11 groups of procedures** spanning the span of nursing care — assessment and observation (vital signs and physical assessment), personal and hygiene care, mobility and safety, nutrition and hydration, elimination care, skin integrity and wound care, infection prevention and control, respiratory care, medicines administration, and care at the end of life, among them. Your programme's skills documents and PAD break each group into the individual procedures you'll be signed off against.

Three practical truths about Annexe B on placement:

1. **Placement type dictates opportunity.** A surgical ward showers you with wound care and post-op observations; community placements are richer in long-term-condition and end-of-life care. Review your outstanding procedures at the start of each placement and tell your supervisor what you're hunting — the [evidence guide](/guides/how-to-evidence-nmc-proficiencies) covers the how.
2. **Simulation can start it; practice usually finishes it.** Skills labs introduce procedures safely, but sign-off standards for real-world performance are set by your programme — know which procedures your university accepts simulated evidence for.
3. **Log the context, not just the tick.** "Administered oral medications under supervision to four patients, including crushed-tablet administration via PEG" is evidence; a date and a checkbox is barely a claim.

## How the annexes are assessed

Same machinery as everything else in your PAD: practice supervisors observe and feed back, your practice assessor signs, your academic assessor confirms progression. The difference is that annexe skills lend themselves to *episodic* assessment — a supervisor can watch you take a full set of vital signs today and judge it — so they're the statements you can most actively engineer opportunities for. Ask early, ask specifically, and get sign-offs recorded on the day rather than reconstructed at the placement's end.

## Frequently asked questions

**How many procedure groups are in Annexe B?**
Eleven, each containing individual procedures your programme assesses. Your university's PAD or skills passport lists the exact breakdown you'll be signed off against.

**Are Annexe A and B statements part of the 219 proficiencies?**
Yes — the 219 statements PlaceMate tracks span the seven platforms plus both annexes. They're all requirements for registration.

**Can Annexe B procedures be signed off in the skills lab?**
Some can be *introduced* or partially evidenced through simulation, within your programme's rules and the NMC's 600-hour simulated-learning cap. Which procedures accept simulated evidence varies by university — check before relying on it.

**Do the annexes apply to all fields of nursing?**
Yes. Like the platforms, Annexe A and B apply across all four fields; your field shapes the contexts in which you demonstrate them.
