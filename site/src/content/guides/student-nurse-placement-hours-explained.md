UK pre-registration nursing students must complete at least **2,300 hours of practice learning** — placement hours — before they can join the NMC register. That sits inside a minimum **4,600-hour programme**, split evenly between practice and theory, and up to **600** of the practice hours may come from simulated learning. This guide explains where those numbers come from, what tends to count, and how to stay on top of your own total from your very first shift.

## How many placement hours do you need for a nursing degree in the UK?

The short answer: **at least 2,300 hours of supervised practice learning**.

The [Nursing and Midwifery Council (NMC)](https://www.nmc.org.uk/standards/standards-for-nurses/) sets the requirements every UK pre-registration nursing programme must meet. A programme must include a minimum of 4,600 hours in total, of which:

- at least **2,300 hours are theory** — lectures, seminars, academic study; and
- at least **2,300 hours are practice learning** — the placements where you learn as part of a real team, supervised, with real people in your care.

Your university designs its own programme around those minimums, so exactly how the hours fall across your course is set by your programme handbook — but the 2,300-hour practice floor is the same for everyone, in every field, at every UK university.

## The 600-hour simulated learning cap

Since the NMC updated its programme standards, universities have been able to deliver **up to 600 of the 2,300 practice hours through simulated practice learning** — skills labs, simulation suites, and structured scenario work.

Two things follow from that cap:

1. **Simulated hours are practice hours** — they count toward your 2,300, so log them.
2. **They're capped** — at least 1,700 of your practice hours must come from real placement settings. If your programme uses a lot of simulation, it's worth tracking the split so you always know how much of each you've banked.

## What counts as placement hours?

The honest answer is: **your university decides the detail**, and its word is final — but the broad shape is consistent across programmes.

Generally counted:

- **Rostered shifts on placement** — days, nights, weekends, long days, including handover.
- **Simulated practice learning** that your programme designates as practice hours (within the 600-hour cap).
- **Structured learning within a placement** — for example spoke days or insight visits arranged as part of the placement.

Generally *not* counted:

- **Travel** to and from placement.
- **Sickness and absence** — hours you miss usually have to be made up before the end of the programme, which is exactly why an accurate running total matters.
- **Unrostered extra time** you stayed out of goodwill, unless your programme says otherwise.

When in doubt, ask your practice assessor or placement team and follow your programme handbook — never a rule of thumb from another university (or another year of the same course).

## How are the hours usually spread across three years?

Most three-year programmes spread practice learning fairly evenly, so students typically complete somewhere in the region of **700–800 practice hours a year**. Your own pattern may front-load theory in first year and lean harder into practice by third year — the only totals that matter in the end are the programme minimums.

A useful habit: rather than thinking "I need 2,300 hours" (which feels enormous), track **hours per placement**. Each placement has an expected hours target; hit each one and the big number takes care of itself.

## How to track your placement hours (and why as-you-go beats catch-up)

Every student nurse has met someone in third year frantically reconstructing shifts from old rotas, text messages and memory. Don't be that person. The reliable method is boring and simple:

1. **Log each shift as you complete it** — date, placement, hours worked (minus breaks if your programme excludes them).
2. **Mark what kind of hours they are** — placement or simulated.
3. **Keep a running total** — per placement and overall, so you always know how far you are from 2,300.
4. **Reconcile weekly** — two minutes at the end of the week against your rota catches errors while they're still easy to fix.
5. **Record made-up hours explicitly** — if you were off sick, note the deficit and when you cleared it.

You can do all of this on paper or a spreadsheet. ## What happens if you fall short?

You can't register with the NMC until your programme confirms you've met the required hours — so a shortfall at the end of third year means making the hours up before you can qualify, which can delay your registration and your first post. Universities have processes for this (retrieval placements, extended programmes), but every one of them is slower and more stressful than simply knowing your numbers all the way through.

The earlier you spot a gap, the cheaper it is to close. That's the whole argument for tracking as you go.

## Frequently asked questions

**Do night shifts and weekends count toward my placement hours?**
Yes — rostered shifts on placement count whatever time of day they fall, including nights, weekends and bank holidays. Log them like any other shift.

**Do simulation hours count as placement hours?**
Yes, up to a point: programmes may count up to 600 hours of simulated practice learning toward the 2,300-hour practice requirement. Beyond the cap, practice hours must come from real placement settings.

**What if I'm off sick during placement?**
Missed hours normally need to be made up — your university will tell you how and when. Record the missed shifts at the time so the deficit is visible rather than a surprise later.

**Is it 2,300 placement hours or 4,600?**
Both numbers are real, and they're different things: 4,600 is the whole programme minimum (theory *and* practice); **2,300 is the practice-learning share** — the placement hours this guide is about.

**Does my part-time healthcare job count?**
No — paid work outside your programme doesn't count toward practice hours, however relevant it feels. Only learning your programme designates as practice counts.
