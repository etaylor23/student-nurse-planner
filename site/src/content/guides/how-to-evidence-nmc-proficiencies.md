Evidencing a proficiency means being able to **show** concrete moments of practice that demonstrate it — not just assert that you've done it. The system that works is simple: know which of the [219 statements](/guides/nmc-proficiencies-and-your-pad) are outstanding, capture moments briefly on the day they happen, link each one to the statements it supports, and arrive at every sign-off conversation with the evidence already organised. Here's each step, plus the sign-off etiquette that makes assessors say yes.

## What actually counts as evidence

Evidence is anything that lets your assessor judge that you can do the thing the statement describes:

- **Episodes of care you delivered or participated in** — the workhorse. "Supported a newly diagnosed diabetic patient with self-injection technique under supervision" evidences statements across several platforms at once.
- **Reflections** — a structured [Gibbs reflection](/guides/how-to-write-a-nursing-reflection) on a meaningful episode shows the reasoning behind your practice, which is exactly what many statements ask for.
- **Procedures performed under supervision** — Annexe B skills, logged with context.
- **Feedback** — from practice supervisors, patients and other professionals.
- **Teaching, audits and projects** — quality-improvement work and peer teaching map surprisingly well to platforms 5 and 6.

Quality beats volume every time. One rich episode, honestly described and linked to the right statements, outweighs ten one-liners saying "did obs round".

## The capture habit: two sentences on the day

Evidence dies in the gap between the moment and the write-up. Close the gap:

1. After any meaningful shift, note **what you did and what it demonstrated** — two sentences, on your phone, before you leave the building.
2. Tag it to the placement and, when you can, the proficiency statements it supports. Don't agonise over perfect mapping in the moment; a rough tag beats no tag.
3. Once a week, tidy: expand anything worth expanding into a proper reflection, and link entries to statements properly.

This is the workflow [PlaceMate](https://app.placemate.uk) is built around — shifts, reflections and skills all link to the specific statements they evidence, and the tracker shows what's still uncovered.

## Work the gaps, not just the flow

Capture-as-you-go covers the statements your placement naturally serves up. The rest need planning:

- **Start each placement with a gap review.** Which statements are outstanding, and which could *this* placement realistically evidence? A community placement is gold for platform 7 (coordinating care); a surgical ward for Annexe B procedures.
- **Tell your supervisor what you need.** "I'm looking for opportunities around discharge planning" is a request any supervisor can act on. Unspoken needs go unmet.
- **Watch the bunching risk.** Statements you avoid (often the leadership ones in platform 5) pile up into final placements. The [platforms guide](/guides/nmc-platforms-explained) helps you spot which lens you're neglecting.

## Getting signed off: who, when and how

Under the NMC's supervision and assessment standards, your **practice supervisors** feed in day-to-day observations, your **practice assessor** signs proficiencies for the placement or part, and your **academic assessor** confirms progression at university level. Practically, that means the person whose signature you need is busy and sees you intermittently — so the sign-off conversation is yours to make easy:

1. **Book it early.** Ask in week one when assessments will happen; never leave sign-offs to the final week.
2. **Bring an agenda.** "I'd like to be assessed on these — here's my evidence for each." A prepared student turns an hour of archaeology into fifteen minutes of confirmation.
3. **Show, don't narrate.** Have the episode, reflection or feedback ready to hand, linked to the statement. This is where an organised tracker pays for itself.
4. **Invite the no.** Ask what's missing for the statements they won't yet sign — that's your plan for the rest of the placement, not a rejection.
5. **Confirm it landed in the PAD.** A sign-off that exists only in conversation doesn't exist.

## Frequently asked questions

**How much evidence do I need per proficiency?**
Enough for your assessor to judge competence confidently — often one strong, well-documented episode, sometimes several across contexts. Your PAD and assessor set the bar; organised evidence lets them set it quickly.

**Can one piece of evidence cover multiple proficiencies?**
Yes, and it should — a rich episode of care typically evidences statements across several platforms. Link it to all of them.

**What if my assessor barely saw me during the placement?**
That's what supervisor feedback is for — your practice assessor is expected to draw on it. Make sure supervisors record feedback as you go, and flag thin assessor contact early rather than at the final interview.

**What if a proficiency is signed off but I don't feel confident?**
Sign-off is a milestone, not a ceiling. Keep seeking the experience — and say so; supervisors respond well to "I'm signed off but want more reps at this."
